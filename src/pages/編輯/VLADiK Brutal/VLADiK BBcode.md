[b][u]INTRODUCTION[/u][/b]
VLADiK Brutal is a solo-developed, indie first-person shooter game, created by Vladislav Meshcheryakov over a span of 5 years. The protagonist, an ex-prisoner named Vlad, is sent to perform a task at a facility. Despite completing his assignment, the reactor overloads, causing an explosion. The game's main action starts here, and it lives up to its name with a strong focus on intensity.

[b][u]THE WORLD AND LORE[/u][/b]

In a fictional nation within Eastern Europe, a long-standing dictator holds power. The ruler, marked by paranoia, has carried out numerous purges, eliminating those in opposition. To monitor common citizens, a tracking chip is implanted in their necks. Everyone wears identical clothing, bearing a unique number, all regulated by the government. Despite these measures, the ruler establishes a Grand Research Facility, utilizing prisoners for nefarious purposes. This country incarcerates many due to resistance against the dictatorship, resulting in executions or imprisonment and exploitation as forced labor. The game's general atmosphere is predominantly gloomy and somber, but the interior settings of structures contrast greatly with their vibrant and colorful palettes.

[b][u]CAMPAIGN[/u][/b]
The game starts with you in prison, talking with your friend Gavryusha about a task you’ve been given. He suggests using the flashlight batteries to make a lighter, but you refuse to avoid getting more time added to your sentence. The story moves quickly, taking you to the "Innovation Center" to help some scientists. But things go wrong, and soon you’re up against a bunch of monsters and military forces. Your main goal is to take down the President and free your country from his oppressive rule.

It's the standard post-soviet shooter, everything is shit, dystopic and hopeless unless you bring the hurt to the ones at the top of the country. 
One thing that bugged me though is the end of the game.

VLADiK features a highly cohesive art direction, with each area feeling logically connected to the next. [b][u]SOUND DESIGN[/u][/b]
The sound design is excellent. From the satisfying gunfire to the eerie ambient audio and the cawing of ravens, the game nails the atmosphere. When you're underground, you might hear speakers repeating warnings or the distant, terrifying screams of rampaging monsters. Gunfights, the screams of men and women, and other chaotic sounds contribute to the tension. The military enemies sound similar to the Combine from Half-Life, with heavily distorted voices. After you kill them, you can hear someone on the radio likely asking for their status, followed by eerie silence.
The voice acting is serviceable, nothing grand since the dev himself is voicing the main character. 

[b][u]ENEMIES[/u][/b]
The game features a variety of enemies, including mutants and military personnel, each presenting unique challenges. Mutants range from [b]standard enemies with tentacles on their faces who rush at you[/b] when they sense your presence, to [b]invisible foes that can be detected by their distinct sounds[/b]. 
There are also [b]naked mutant women[/b] who flee from you but spawn [b]kamikaze children[/b] that explode on impact, with the spawning stopping only when the "Mom" is killed. 
[b]Prowlers[/b], crawling on all fours, are known for their jumping attacks, while [b]fat slobs[/b] move slowly but can deal significant damage if they get too close, though they can usually be taken down with two shotgun blasts.

The military faction adds another layer of difficulty with [b]standard goons armed with pistols[/b], [b]baton-wielding goons[/b] who rush at you, and [b]commandos [/b]who come in two types: [b]green with assault rifles[/b] and [b]blue with shotguns[/b], both fast and aggressive. [b]Riot Shield Goons[/b] are slower and try to protect their allies by walking in front of them, but they’re easy to deal with once you figure out their pattern. [b]Heavies[/b], heavily armored and equipped with machine guns, can absorb a lot of damage and deal significant firepower. [b]Attack dogs[/b] track and attack you, [b]snipers [/b]require careful navigation to avoid their long-range shots, and in the final missions, you face two [b]assassins [/b]equipped with jetpacks, making them elusive and difficult to handle as they dodge and get close. Together, these enemies create a challenging and varied experience throughout the game.

It's a nice variety of enemies, though I'd say the military are the more difficult ones to fight against since they have guns and run just as fast as you do, they also like to flank you or run behind you. A nice plus is that there are Riot Shields which are used cleverly, meaning that two ranged goons stand behind it while firing at you.

The game offers a diverse arsenal of weapons suited for various combat scenarios. The 6-shooter revolver is a powerful sidearm with limited ammo, capable of one-shotting most enemies. The Regulator fires electrical orbs that attract and then explode, making it effective for crowd control. For close-range combat, the pump shotgun stands out as a particularly strong choice, while the AS Val provides a superior alternative to the AK with its 30-bullet magazine. The AK itself, with its 45-bullet magazine, remains a reliable and efficient weapon. The two-barrel shotgun delivers significant firepower, especially against heavily armored foes. For explosive needs, the grenade detonates on impact, and the Molotov cocktails are ideal for trapping enemies in confined spaces. The single-shot sniper can eliminate most foes with one hit, though it struggles with heavies. The RPG-7 is invaluable for taking down large groups and helicopter bosses. Additionally, the Tokarev pistol provides a dependable option for dealing with lesser threats. Each weapon offers unique strengths

From all the weapons that are ingame, I used the pump shotgun the most. It is a fantastic close-range weapon that kills most enemies with 1-2 shots, and is even useful middle-range. The Regulator was also quite fun to use since it just sucks all enemies to it and then explodes. You usually see a bunch of limbs and blood flying around when that happens, fun times! [b][u]NEGATIVES & POSITIVES[/u][/b]
Many of the game’s negatives can be easily adjusted through in-game options. Features like head bobbing, weapon sway, blur, bloom, and other post-processing effects can be turned off to improve comfort. On the positive side, the game offers a challenging experience without being overly frustrating. However, you do need to reload your game manually after each death, so frequent saving is advisable. While the game does include auto-saves, they are often placed at the beginning of fights, which can be inconvenient. Despite this, the game is solid overall, with few significant complaints.

[b][u]CONCLUSION[/u][/b]
Overall, VLADiK Brutal is a solid game, especially considering it was made by one person over five years. It's got that gritty, post-Soviet vibe down, and has some intense action, a bunch of cool russian guns and a good mix of enemies trying to fuck you up. The Atmosphere in each level feels great, the gunplay is satisfying, even if some weapons are clearly better than others.
