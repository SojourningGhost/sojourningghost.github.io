True Image of a Living Creature Unseen in Past and Present, the Fierce Tiger

Without entering the tiger's den, difficult it is to obtain the tiger's cub.
Verily, in any business, a man of that path is wisest.
In the woods of the deep mountain called Helsiyanat of Atlantic Holland*, always there live fierce tigers,
and most rare indeed is the wood cutter or the like who, having gone, returns.
When their wives, children, and others worry deeply and many were they who grieve morning and evening,
one day a strange old man of youthful face and crane-white hair suddenly appears in the village
and saying to those wives and children, "Your fathers and husbands
in the midst of Helsiyana** lost their lives because of the fierce tigers," informs them.
The wives, children, and others hear this and are greatly amazed, collapsing on the road and pressed with lamentation.
Thus, the old man teaches the hunters and others of the village a technique by which to catch a fierce tiger alive.
When the hunters, according to the teaching of the aged man, catch two tigers, one large and one small,
the wives, children, and so on of those who had finished their lives all together slay one, each avenging the foe of their fathers and husbands.
A man called Trader Christiaan sought to redeem one tiger cub and,
in the beginning of the imperial Man'en era, year of elder metal and monkey, late May,
bringing it with him, crossed over to the new port Yokohama of Great Japan; how strange!
To view without sojourning a fierce tiger unseen in past and present
is truly the blessing of a great peace
and a thing to revere and honor.

by gossip officer Kanagaki Robun

at request, painted from life by Kyousai

\*It is not clear to me where this actually is. In the Edo Period, knowledge from the West was called generally ｜蘭学《Hollandology》, and Japanese knowledge of geography outside East Asia was understandably poor.
\*\*Yes, it's lacking 't' the second time.

An online acquaintance used OCR on this print to woefully inaccurate results, so I decided to transcribe it. I was already nearly finished when I discovered that trasncriptions already existed online. I ended up consulting one to make sense of a few particularly bizarre 変体仮名, but all else was transcribed under my own power, including most of the 変体仮名. Fortunately for me, the 漢字 themselves were not overly stylized, though many were written according to old or alternate forms.

Having transcribed it, it seemed a shame to not also translate it. As is my usual habit, I did my best to stay faithful to the original wording, tense, and so on, though the line breaks were rearranged for my own convenience. Such abundant use of the historical present in writing sounds strange to Anglophone ears, but I think that, when poetry and lyricism is not a concern, it is good to convey the details of the original writing as much as can be done across a language barrier. To address some translation choices:

* I have seen others translate 猛虎 as *wild tiger*, which may happen to be an accurate description of the tigers in this case, but 猛 unquestionably means fierce, not wild (as in *wild animal*).
* 異翁 is a rare term that doesn't appear even in Japanese dictionaries. Those described as such seem to land somewhere between mysterious and supernatural. I eventually settled on calqueing it with *a strange old man*, hoping to evoke both the *foreign* and *unusual* senses of *strange*.
* 等 is hard to maintain in translation. You can see where I made it 'or the like' and 'others' in various places. English lacks an easy suffix to indicate the same thing.
* 悲歎にせまる presented some difficulty, first in understanding which use of せまる was intended, and then in trying to convey the same idea of constrained breathing and a constricted chest succinctly in English. I am quite pleased to have thought of *pressed*; I think it works very well.
* Christiaan is my best guess as to his original name, assuming that he is Dutch. It seemed the closest thing to ｜コレセシヤアン《Koreseshiyaan》. However, I am not well versed in Dutch names, so it is possible I missed something else that would make sense.
* When translating the year of the sexagenary cycle, I decided to base it on the Japanese reading of 庚, not the Chinese one; hence, *elder metal* instead of *yang metal*.
* 太平 means the 'world' (Japan) is peaceful. I couldn't find an exact equivalent in English, so I added *great* (which also nicely matches 太) before *peace* to indicate more than a local or interpersonal peace.
* The 稗官, or 'gossip officer', as I have translated it, was a minor official in ancient China that gathered the gossip and sayings of the people and reported them to the king. Kanagaki was presumably using the term facetiously.
